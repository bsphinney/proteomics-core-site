# Capacity Utilization: UC Davis Proteomics Core Facility

**Generated March 25, 2026** | Data source: Stratocore LIMS, staff workload records

## Utilization Status: At Capacity

The UC Davis Proteomics Core Facility operates at full capacity. The primary constraint is instrumentation: of three mass spectrometers, only one — the Bruker timsTOF HT — represents current-generation technology. The remaining two aging instruments limit both throughput and the range of workflows the facility can offer. Demand for time on the timsTOF HT consistently exceeds comfortable scheduling margins.

### Instrument Configuration

| Instrument | Status | Role |
|------------|--------|------|
| Bruker timsTOF HT | Current-generation | Primary workhorse — dia-PASEF, 4D proteomics, high-throughput discovery |
| Thermo Fusion Lumos | Aging | DDA/DIA workflows; declining reliability, increasing maintenance |
| Thermo Exploris 480 | Aging | Backup/overflow; limited sensitivity vs. current platforms |

The timsTOF HT carries a disproportionate share of analytical demand. Investigators requiring state-of-the-art sensitivity or throughput — including single-cell proteomics, large clinical cohorts, and deep phosphoproteomics — depend entirely on this single instrument.

### Demand Trajectory

The core is not currently turning away work; however, multiple indicators show demand pressing against capacity limits:

- **Growing external reach:** Non-UC Davis academic users now account for 35% of revenue, up from 16% in FY2024 — a doubling in two years that adds instrument-time pressure competing with internal users.
- **Broad user base:** 320 unique PI groups from 104+ institutions have used the facility over three years, with 60–80 new groups joining each year.
- **Persistent queue:** 62 orders currently await completion.
- **Single modern instrument:** All demand for cutting-edge proteomics workflows funnels through one timsTOF HT, creating a single point of failure and a scheduling bottleneck.

### Conclusion

The facility operates at capacity with a single modern mass spectrometer and three analytical staff members carrying full workloads. Acquisition of a new high-performance instrument would directly expand analytical throughput, reduce dependence on aging platforms, eliminate the single-point-of-failure risk, and enable advanced experimental capabilities that the growing user base demands. The sustained breadth of usage — 320 PI groups across 104+ institutions — demonstrates that additional capacity would be immediately utilized.
